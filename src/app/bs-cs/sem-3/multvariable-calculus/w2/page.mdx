# Week 2 - Partial Derivatives

**Book:** *Multivariable Calculus* by James Stewart, 9th Edition
**Chapter:** 14. Partial Derivatives
**Section:** 14.3 Partial Derivatives
**Exercises:** 14.3

---

### Definition

If $f$ is a function of two variables, its partial derivatives are the functions $f_x$ and $f_y$ defined by

<MathWrapper>
$$
f_x(x, y) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h, y) - f(x, y)}{h}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<MathWrapper>
$$
f_y(x, y) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x, y + h) - f(x, y)}{h}
$$
</MathWrapper>

### Explanation (Rule for Finding Partial Derivatives)

1.  To find $f_x$, regard $y$ as a constant and differentiate $f(x, y)$ with respect to $x$.
2.  To find $f_y$, regard $x$ as a constant and differentiate $f(x, y)$ with respect to $y$.

---

### Solved Examples from the Book

**Example 1:** If $f(x, y) = x^3 + x^2y^3 - 2y^2$, find $f_x(2, 1)$ and $f_y(2, 1)$.

**Solution:**
Holding $y$ constant and differentiating with respect to $x$, we get
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_x(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy^3
$$
</MathWrapper>
and so
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_x(2, 1) = 3(2)^2 + 2(2)(1)^3 = 16
$$
</MathWrapper>
Holding $x$ constant and differentiating with respect to $y$, we get
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_y(x, y) = 3x^2y^2 - 4y
$$
</MathWrapper>
and so
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_y(2, 1) = 3(2)^2(1)^2 - 4(1) = 8
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**Example 2:** If $f(x, y) = \sin\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right)$, calculate $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$.

**Solution:**
Using the Chain Rule for functions of one variable, we have:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= \cos\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right) \\
&= \cos\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{1 + y}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} &= \cos\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right) \\
&= \cos\left(\frac{x}{1 + y}\right) \cdot \left(-\frac{x}{(1 + y)^2}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

### Exercise 14.3 Solutions

**10.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
f(x, y) = x^2y - 3y^4
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_x(x, y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2y - 3y^4) = 2xy - 0 = 2xy
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_y(x, y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2y - 3y^4) = x^2 - 12y^3
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**11.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
g(x, y) = x^3 \sin y
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
g_x(x, y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3 \sin y) = 3x^2 \sin y
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
g_y(x, y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^3 \sin y) = x^3 \cos y
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**16.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
g(x, y) = (x^2 + xy)^3
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_x(x, y) &= 3(x^2 + xy)^2 \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2 + xy) \\
&= 3(x^2 + xy)^2 (2x + y)
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_y(x, y) &= 3(x^2 + xy)^2 \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2 + xy) \\
&= 3(x^2 + xy)^2 (0 + x) \\
&= 3x(x^2 + xy)^2
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**19.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
f(x, y) = \frac{ax + by}{cx + dy}
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_x(x, y) &= \frac{(cx + dy)(a) - (ax + by)(c)}{(cx + dy)^2} \\
&= \frac{acx + ady - acx - bcy}{(cx + dy)^2} \\
&= \frac{ady - bcy}{(cx + dy)^2}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
f_y(x, y) &= \frac{(cx + dy)(b) - (ax + by)(d)}{(cx + dy)^2} \\
&= \frac{bcx + bdy - adx - bdy}{(cx + dy)^2} \\
&= \frac{bcx - adx}{(cx + dy)^2}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**22.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
u(r, \theta) = \sin(r \cos \theta)
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial u}{\partial r} &= \cos(r \cos \theta) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r \cos \theta) \\
&= \cos(r \cos \theta) \cdot (\cos \theta)
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta} &= \cos(r \cos \theta) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(r \cos \theta) \\
&= \cos(r \cos \theta) \cdot (r (-\sin \theta)) \\
&= -r \sin \theta \cos(r \cos \theta)
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**27.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
f(x, y, z) = x^3 y z^2 + 2yz
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_x = 3x^2 y z^2 + 0 = 3x^2 y z^2
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_y = x^3 z^2 + 2z
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_z = x^3 y (2z) + 2y = 2x^3 y z + 2y
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**30.** Find the first partial derivatives of the function.
<MathWrapper>
$$
w = y \tan(x + 2z)
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
\frac{\partial w}{\partial x} = y \sec^2(x + 2z) \cdot (1) = y \sec^2(x + 2z)
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\frac{\partial w}{\partial y} = 1 \cdot \tan(x + 2z) = \tan(x + 2z)
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\frac{\partial w}{\partial z} = y \sec^2(x + 2z) \cdot (2) = 2y \sec^2(x + 2z)
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**41.** Use implicit differentiation to find $\partial z / \partial x$ and $\partial z / \partial y$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
x^2 + 2y^2 + 3z^2 = 1
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
To find $\partial z / \partial x$, differentiate implicitly with respect to $x$ (treating $y$ as constant):
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2y^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3z^2) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(1) \\
2x + 0 + 6z \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} &= 0 \\
6z \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} &= -2x \\
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} &= -\frac{2x}{6z} = -\frac{x}{3z}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

To find $\partial z / \partial y$, differentiate implicitly with respect to $y$ (treating $x$ as constant):
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2y^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3z^2) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(1) \\
0 + 4y + 6z \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} &= 0 \\
6z \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} &= -4y \\
\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} &= -\frac{4y}{6z} = -\frac{2y}{3z}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**47.** Find all the second partial derivatives.
<MathWrapper>
$$
f(x, y) = x^4 y - 2x^3 y^2
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
First partial derivatives:
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_x = 4x^3 y - 6x^2 y^2
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_y = x^4 - 4x^3 y
$$
</MathWrapper>
Second partial derivatives:
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_{xx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(4x^3 y - 6x^2 y^2) = 12x^2 y - 12x y^2
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(4x^3 y - 6x^2 y^2) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 y
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^4 - 4x^3 y) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 y
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
f_{yy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^4 - 4x^3 y) = 0 - 4x^3 = -4x^3
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br/>

**53.** Verify that the conclusion of Clairaut's Theorem holds, that is, $u_{xy} = u_{yx}$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
u = x^4 y^3 - y^4
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
u_x = 4x^3 y^3 - 0 = 4x^3 y^3
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
u_y = 3x^4 y^2 - 4y^3
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
u_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(u_x) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(4x^3 y^3) = 12x^3 y^2
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
u_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(u_y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3x^4 y^2 - 4y^3) = 12x^3 y^2
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
u_{xy} = u_{yx}
$$
</MathWrapper>
