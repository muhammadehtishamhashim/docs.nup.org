### Definitions

**Boundary-Value Problem (BVP)**  
A problem consisting of solving a linear differential equation of order two or greater in which the dependent variable $y$ or its derivatives are specified at different points is called a boundary-value problem. For a second-order equation, the boundary conditions are often specified at two points $x=a$ and $x=b$.

$$
a_2(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + a_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + a_0(x)y = g(x)
$$
$$
\text{Subject to: } y(a) = y_0, \quad y(b) = y_1
$$

<br />

**Eigenvalues and Eigenfunctions**  
In many applied problems, we encounter a BVP involving a parameter $\lambda$:
$$
y'' + \lambda y = 0, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y(L) = 0
$$
The values of $\lambda$ for which the boundary-value problem has **nontrivial** (nonzero) solutions are called **eigenvalues**. The corresponding nontrivial solutions are called **eigenfunctions**. These functions form the basis for Fourier Series.

---

### Explanation

Unlike initial-value problems (IVPs) where conditions are given at a single point (e.g., $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$) and a unique solution is guaranteed, boundary-value problems can have:
1.  A unique solution.
2.  No solution.
3.  Infinitely many solutions.

The study of eigenvalues in BVPs is crucial for topics like Fourier Series, as it determines the fundamental frequencies (or modes) of a physical system, such as a vibrating string or a buckling column.

### Solved Examples from the Book

**Example 1: A BVP Can Have Many, One, or No Solutions (Sec 4.1, Ex 3)**  
**Problem:** Analyze the solutions of $x'' + 16x = 0$ subject to different boundary conditions.  
**Solution:**  
The general solution is:
$$
x(t) = c_1 \cos 4t + c_2 \sin 4t
$$

**(a) Subject to $x(0) = 0, x(\pi/2) = 0$:**
$$
x(0) = c_1(1) + c_2(0) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0
$$
$$
x(t) = c_2 \sin 4t
$$
$$
x(\pi/2) = c_2 \sin(4 \cdot \pi/2) = c_2 \sin(2\pi) = 0
$$
This is satisfied for **any** constant $c_2$. Thus, there are **infinitely many solutions**.

<br />

**(b) Subject to $x(0) = 0, x(\pi/8) = 0$:**
$$
x(0) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0
$$
$$
x(\pi/8) = c_2 \sin(4 \cdot \pi/8) = c_2 \sin(\pi/2) = c_2(1) = 0 \implies c_2 = 0
$$
Thus, $x = 0$ is the **only solution**.

<br />

**(c) Subject to $x(0) = 0, x(\pi/2) = 1$:**
$$
x(0) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0
$$
$$
x(\pi/2) = c_2 \sin(2\pi) = 0
$$
Since the condition requires $x(\pi/2) = 1$ and $0 \ne 1$, the BVP has **no solution**.

<br />

**Example 2: An Embedded Beam (Sec 5.2, Ex 1)**  
**Problem:** A beam of length $L$ is embedded at both ends. Find the deflection $y(x)$ if a constant load $w_0$ is uniformly distributed.  
**Differential Equation:** $EI \frac{d^4y}{dx^4} = w_0$  
**Boundary Conditions:** $y(0)=0, y'(0)=0$ and $y(L)=0, y'(L)=0$  
**Solution:**  
Integrating four times:
$$
y(x) = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2 + c_4 x^3 + \frac{w_0}{24EI}x^4
$$
Applying $y(0) = 0$ and $y'(0) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0, c_2 = 0$.  
Applying conditions at $x=L$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
y(L) &= c_3 L^2 + c_4 L^3 + \frac{w_0}{24EI}L^4 = 0 \\
y'(L) &= 2c_3 L + 3c_4 L^2 + \frac{w_0}{6EI}L^3 = 0
\end{aligned}
$$
Solving the system:
$$
c_3 = \frac{w_0 L^2}{24EI}, \quad c_4 = -\frac{w_0 L}{12EI}
$$
**Answer:** $y(x) = \frac{w_0}{24EI} x^2(x-L)^2$

<br />

**Example 3: Nontrivial Solutions of a BVP (Sec 5.2, Ex 2)**  
**Problem:** Solve $y'' + \lambda y = 0, y(0) = 0, y(L) = 0$.  
**Solution:**  
**Case I ($\lambda = 0$):** $y = c_1 x + c_2$.  
$y(0) = 0, y(L) = 0 \implies c_1=0, c_2=0$ (Trivial).  
<br />
**Case II ($\lambda < 0$):** Let $\lambda = -\alpha^2$. $y = c_1 \cosh \alpha x + c_2 \sinh \alpha x$.  
$y(0) = 0, y(L) = 0 \implies c_1=0, c_2=0$ (Trivial).  
<br />
**Case III ($\lambda > 0$):** Let $\lambda = \alpha^2$. $y = c_1 \cos \alpha x + c_2 \sin \alpha x$.  
$y(0) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0$.  
$y(L) = c_2 \sin \alpha L = 0$. For nontrivial solutions:
$$
\alpha L = n\pi \implies \alpha = \frac{n\pi}{L} \implies \lambda_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2}{L^2}
$$
**Eigenfunctions:** $y_n(x) = \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$ for $n=1, 2, 3, \dots$

### Solved Exercise Questions

**1. (Ex 4.1, Q11)** Solve $y'' - y = 0$ with $y(0) = 0, y(1) = 1$ using $y = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{-x}$.  
**Solution:**  
$$
y(0) = c_1 + c_2 = 0 \implies c_2 = -c_1
$$
$$
y(x) = c_1(e^x - e^{-x}) = 2c_1 \sinh x
$$
$$
y(1) = 2c_1 \sinh(1) = 1 \implies c_1 = \frac{1}{2\sinh 1}
$$
**Answer:** $y = \frac{\sinh x}{\sinh 1}$

<br />

**2. (Ex 4.1, Q13)** Solve $y = c_1 e^x \cos x + c_2 e^x \sin x$ for $y(0)=1, y'(\pi)=0$.  
**Solution:**  
$$
y(0) = c_1(1) + 0 = 1 \implies c_1 = 1
$$
$$
y'(x) = e^x[(1+c_2)\cos x + (c_2-1)\sin x]
$$
$$
y'(\pi) = e^\pi[(1+c_2)(-1) + 0] = 0 \implies 1+c_2 = 0 \implies c_2 = -1
$$
**Answer:** $y = e^x(\cos x - \sin x)$

<br />

**3. (Ex 5.2, Q1)** Find deflection $y(x)$ for a beam embedded at $x=0$ and free at $x=L$ with $w(x) = w_0$.  
**Solution:**  
$$
y(x) = c_3 x^2 + c_4 x^3 + \frac{w_0}{24EI}x^4 \quad (\text{since } y(0)=0, y'(0)=0)
$$
Conditions at $x=L$ (Free end): $y''(L)=0, y'''(L)=0$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
y'''(L) &= 6c_4 + \frac{w_0 L}{EI} = 0 \implies c_4 = -\frac{w_0 L}{6EI} \\
y''(L) &= 2c_3 + 6c_4 L + \frac{w_0 L^2}{2EI} = 0 \implies c_3 = \frac{w_0 L^2}{4EI}
\end{aligned}
$$
**Answer:** $y(x) = \frac{w_0}{24EI}(6L^2x^2 - 4Lx^3 + x^4)$

<br />

**4. (Ex 5.2, Q9)** Solve $y'' + \lambda y = 0, y(0)=0, y(\pi)=0$.  
**Solution:**  
$$
y(0)=0 \implies c_1=0 \implies y = c_2 \sin \sqrt{\lambda}x
$$
$$
y(\pi) = \sin(\sqrt{\lambda}\pi) = 0 \implies \sqrt{\lambda} = n
$$
**Answer:** $\lambda_n = n^2, \quad y_n = \sin nx$

<br />

**5. (Ex 5.2, Q11)** Solve $y'' + \lambda y = 0, y'(0)=0, y(L)=0$.  
**Solution:**  
$$
y'(0) = 0 \implies c_2 = 0 \implies y = c_1 \cos \sqrt{\lambda}x
$$
$$
y(L) = \cos(\sqrt{\lambda}L) = 0 \implies \sqrt{\lambda}L = \frac{(2n-1)\pi}{2}
$$
**Answer:** $\lambda_n = \frac{(2n-1)^2\pi^2}{4L^2}, \quad y_n = \cos\left(\frac{(2n-1)\pi x}{2L}\right)$

<br />

**6. (Ex 5.2, Q17)** Solve $x^2y'' + xy' + \lambda y = 0, y(1)=0, y(e^\pi)=0$.  
**Solution:**  
Cauchy-Euler equation solution: $y = c_1 \cos(\sqrt{\lambda} \ln x) + c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\lambda} \ln x)$.
$$
y(1) = c_1 \cos(0) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0
$$
$$
y(e^\pi) = c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\lambda} \ln e^\pi) = c_2 \sin(\pi\sqrt{\lambda}) = 0 \implies \sqrt{\lambda} = n
$$
**Answer:** $\lambda_n = n^2, \quad y_n = \sin(n \ln x)$
