# Line and Surface Integrals

## 1. Line Integral of Vector Field

---

### Definition

Let $\mathbf{F}$ be a continuous vector field defined on a smooth curve $C$ given by a vector function $\mathbf{r}(t)$, $a \le t \le b$. Then the **line integral of $\mathbf{F}$ along $C$** is

<MathWrapper>
$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) \, dt = \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{T} \, ds
$$
</MathWrapper>

### Explanation (Work)

Recall that the work done by a variable force $f(x)$ in moving a particle from $a$ to $b$ along the $x$-axis is $W = \int_a^b f(x) dx$. Then in Section 12.3 we found that the work done by a constant force $\mathbf{F}$ in moving an object from a point $P$ to another point $Q$ in space is $W = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{D}$, where $\mathbf{D} = \vec{PQ}$ is the displacement vector.

Now suppose that $\mathbf{F} = P \mathbf{i} + Q \mathbf{j} + R \mathbf{k}$ is a continuous force field on $\mathbb{R}^3$. We wish to compute the work done by this force in moving a particle along a smooth curve $C$.

The work $W$ done by the force field $\mathbf{F}$ is the limit of the Riemann sums:

<MathWrapper>
$$
W = \int_C \mathbf{F}(x, y, z) \cdot \mathbf{T}(x, y, z) \, ds = \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{T} \, ds
$$
</MathWrapper>

Equation 12 says that work is the line integral with respect to arc length of the tangential component of the force.

### Solved Examples from the Book

**Example 7:** Find the work done by the force field $\mathbf{F}(x, y) = x^2 \mathbf{i} - xy \mathbf{j}$ in moving a particle along the quarter-circle $\mathbf{r}(t) = \cos t \mathbf{i} + \sin t \mathbf{j}$, $0 \le t \le \pi/2$.

**Solution:**
Since $x = \cos t$ and $y = \sin t$, we have
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \cos^2 t \mathbf{i} - \cos t \sin t \mathbf{j}
$$
</MathWrapper>
and
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}'(t) = -\sin t \mathbf{i} + \cos t \mathbf{j}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Therefore the work done is
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} &= \int_0^{\pi/2} \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^{\pi/2} (\cos^2 t \mathbf{i} - \cos t \sin t \mathbf{j}) \cdot (-\sin t \mathbf{i} + \cos t \mathbf{j}) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^{\pi/2} (-\cos^2 t \sin t - \cos^2 t \sin t) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^{\pi/2} (-2 \cos^2 t \sin t) \, dt \\
&= 2 \left[ \frac{\cos^3 t}{3} \right]_0^{\pi/2} \\
&= 2 \left( 0 - \frac{1}{3} \right) \\
&= -\frac{2}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br />

**Example 8:** Evaluate $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$, where $\mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = xy \mathbf{i} + yz \mathbf{j} + zx \mathbf{k}$ and $C$ is the twisted cubic given by
<MathWrapper>
$$
x = t \quad y = t^2 \quad z = t^3 \quad 0 \le t \le 1
$$
</MathWrapper>

**Solution:**
We have
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = t \mathbf{i} + t^2 \mathbf{j} + t^3 \mathbf{k}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}'(t) = \mathbf{i} + 2t \mathbf{j} + 3t^2 \mathbf{k}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = (t)(t^2) \mathbf{i} + (t^2)(t^3) \mathbf{j} + (t^3)(t) \mathbf{k} = t^3 \mathbf{i} + t^5 \mathbf{j} + t^4 \mathbf{k}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Thus
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} &= \int_0^1 \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^1 (t^3 \mathbf{i} + t^5 \mathbf{j} + t^4 \mathbf{k}) \cdot (\mathbf{i} + 2t \mathbf{j} + 3t^2 \mathbf{k}) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^1 (t^3(1) + t^5(2t) + t^4(3t^2)) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^1 (t^3 + 2t^6 + 3t^6) \, dt \\
&= \int_0^1 (t^3 + 5t^6) \, dt \\
&= \left[ \frac{t^4}{4} + \frac{5t^7}{7} \right]_0^1 \\
&= \frac{1}{4} + \frac{5}{7} \\
&= \frac{27}{28}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

**29.** Find the work done by the force field $\mathbf{F}$ in moving an object from $P$ to $Q$.
<MathWrapper>

<MathWrapper>

W = f(2, 2) - f(1, 0) = \left( \frac{2^4}{4} + \frac{2^4}{4} \right) - \left( \frac{1^4}{4} + 0 \right)
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>

$$

## 2. Surface Integrals

**Book:** *Multivariable Calculus* by James Stewart, 9th Edition
**Chapter:** 16. Vector Calculus
**Section:** 16.7 Surface Integrals

---

### Definition

If $S$ is a surface parametrized by a vector function $\mathbf{r}(u, v) = \langle x(u, v), y(u, v), z(u, v) \rangle$ on a domain $D$, the **surface integral of $f$ over $S$** is given by:

<MathWrapper>
$$
\iint_S f(x, y, z) \, dS = \iint_D f(\mathbf{r}(u, v)) |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| \, dA
$$
</MathWrapper>

where $\mathbf{r}_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v$ are the partial derivatives of $\mathbf{r}$ with respect to $u$ and $v$.

**For graphs of functions:** If $S$ is the graph of $z = g(x, y)$, we let $x$ and $y$ be the parameters. Then:

<MathWrapper>
$$
\iint_S f(x, y, z) \, dS = \iint_D f(x, y, g(x, y)) \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2 + 1} \, dA
$$
</MathWrapper>

---

### Solved Examples from the Book

**Example 1:** Compute the surface integral $\iint_S x^2 \, dS$, where $S$ is the unit sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$.

**Solution:**
We use the parametric representation of the sphere using spherical coordinates (with $\rho = 1$):
<MathWrapper>
$$
x = \sin \phi \cos \theta, \quad y = \sin \phi \sin \theta, \quad z = \cos \phi
$$
</MathWrapper>
The domain $D$ is $0 \le \phi \le \pi$ and $0 \le \theta \le 2\pi$.
The cross product of the tangent vectors is given by $|\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = \sin \phi$ (since $\rho = 1$, the factor is $1^2 \sin \phi$).
Using the formula for surface integrals:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_S x^2 \, dS &= \iint_D (\sin \phi \cos \theta)^2 |\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| \, dA \\
&= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi \sin^2 \phi \cos^2 \theta \sin \phi \, d\phi \, d\theta \\
&= \int_0^{2\pi} \cos^2 \theta \, d\theta \int_0^\pi \sin^3 \phi \, d\phi
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Evaluate the integrals separately:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\int_0^{2\pi} \cos^2 \theta \, d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2\theta) \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \theta + \frac{1}{2}\sin 2\theta \right]_0^{2\pi} = \pi
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^\pi \sin^3 \phi \, d\phi &= \int_0^\pi (1 - \cos^2 \phi) \sin \phi \, d\phi \\
&= \left[ -\cos \phi + \frac{\cos^3 \phi}{3} \right]_0^\pi \\
&= \left( -(-1) + \frac{-1}{3} \right) - \left( -1 + \frac{1}{3} \right) = \frac{4}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\text{Total} = \pi \cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{4\pi}{3}
$$
</MathWrapper>

<br />

**Example 2:** Evaluate $\iint_S y \, dS$, where $S$ is the surface $z = x + y^2$, $0 \le x \le 1$, $0 \le y \le 2$.

**Solution:**
Here $S$ is the graph of $z = g(x, y) = x + y^2$. We use the formula for graphs:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 1, \quad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = 2y
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
dS = \sqrt{1^2 + (2y)^2 + 1} \, dA = \sqrt{1 + 4y^2 + 1} \, dA = \sqrt{2 + 4y^2} \, dA
$$
</MathWrapper>
The domain $D$ is the rectangle $0 \le x \le 1$, $0 \le y \le 2$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_S y \, dS &= \int_0^1 \int_0^2 y \sqrt{2 + 4y^2} \, dy \, dx \\
&= \int_0^1 dx \int_0^2 y \sqrt{2 + 4y^2} \, dy
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
For the inner integral, let $u = 2 + 4y^2$, then $du = 8y \, dy \Rightarrow y \, dy = \frac{1}{8} du$.
Limits: $y=0 \to u=2$, $y=2 \to u=18$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^2 y (2 + 4y^2)^{1/2} \, dy &= \frac{1}{8} \int_2^{18} u^{1/2} \, du \\
&= \frac{1}{8} \left[ \frac{2}{3} u^{3/2} \right]_2^{18} \\
&= \frac{1}{12} (18\sqrt{18} - 2\sqrt{2}) \\
&= \frac{1}{12} (54\sqrt{2} - 2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{52\sqrt{2}}{12} = \frac{13\sqrt{2}}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Integrating with respect to $x$ gives just the constant times $(1 - 0)$:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\iint_S y \, dS = \frac{13\sqrt{2}}{3}
$$
</MathWrapper>

**5.** Evaluate $\iint_S (x + y + z) \, dS$, where $S$ is the parallelogram with parametric equations $x = u + v$, $y = u - v$, $z = 1 + 2u + v$, $0 \le u \le 2$, $0 \le v \le 1$.

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}(u, v) = \langle u + v, u - v, 1 + 2u + v \rangle
$$
</MathWrapper>
Compute tangent vectors:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}_u = \langle 1, 1, 2 \rangle
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}_v = \langle 1, -1, 1 \rangle
$$
</MathWrapper>
Compute cross product:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \langle 1 - (-2), 2 - 1, -1 - 1 \rangle = \langle 3, 1, -2 \rangle
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| = \sqrt{3^2 + 1^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 1 + 4} = \sqrt{14}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Substitute into the integral ($f = x+y+z$):
<MathWrapper>
$$
x + y + z = (u+v) + (u-v) + (1+2u+v) = 4u + v + 1
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_S (x+y+z) \, dS &= \int_0^1 \int_0^2 (4u + v + 1) \sqrt{14} \, du \, dv \\
&= \sqrt{14} \int_0^1 \left[ 2u^2 + vu + u \right]_0^2 \, dv \\
&= \sqrt{14} \int_0^1 (8 + 2v + 2) \, dv \\
&= \sqrt{14} \int_0^1 (10 + 2v) \, dv \\
&= \sqrt{14} \left[ 10v + v^2 \right]_0^1 \\
&= \sqrt{14} (10 + 1) = 11\sqrt{14}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

**6.** Evaluate $\iint_S xyz \, dS$, where $S$ is the cone with parametric equations $x = u \cos v$, $y = u \sin v$, $z = u$, $0 \le u \le 1$, $0 \le v \le \pi/2$.

**Solution:**
<MathWrapper>
$$
\mathbf{r}_u = \langle \cos v, \sin v, 1 \rangle
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>

\mathbf{r}_v = \langle -u \sin v, u \cos v, 0 \rangle

</MathWrapper>

$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
dS = \sqrt{(-2)^2 + (-2)^2 + 1} \, dA = \sqrt{4+4+1} \, dA = 3 \, dA
$$
</MathWrapper>
Region $D$: $z \ge 0 \Rightarrow 4 - 2x - 2y \ge 0 \Rightarrow x + y \le 2$. First octant means $x \ge 0, y \ge 0$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
D = \{(x, y) | 0 \le x \le 2, 0 \le y \le 2 - x\}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Integral:
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_D x(4 - 2x - 2y) \cdot 3 \, dA &= 3 \int_0^2 \int_0^{2-x} (4x - 2x^2 - 2xy) \, dy \, dx \\
&= 3 \int_0^2 \left[ 4xy - 2x^2y - xy^2 \right]_0^{2-x} \, dx \\
&= 3 \int_0^2 \left( 4x(2-x) - 2x^2(2-x) - x(2-x)^2 \right) \, dx \\
&= 3 \int_0^2 (2-x) \left( 4x - 2x^2 - x(2-x) \right) \, dx \\
&= 3 \int_0^2 (2-x) (4x - 2x^2 - 2x + x^2) \, dx \\
&= 3 \int_0^2 (2-x)(2x - x^2) \, dx \\
&= 3 \int_0^2 x(2-x)^2 \, dx
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Let $u = 2-x$, $du = -dx$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
= 3 \int_2^0 (2-u)u^2 (-du) = 3 \int_0^2 (2u^2 - u^3) \, du = 3 \left[ \frac{2}{3}u^3 - \frac{u^4}{4} \right]_0^2 = 3 \left( \frac{16}{3} - 4 \right) = 16 - 12 = 4
$$
</MathWrapper>
**Answer:** $4$

**11.** Evaluate $\iint_S x \, dS$, $S$ is the triangular region with vertices $(1, 0, 0)$, $(0, -2, 0)$, and $(0, 0, 4)$.

**Solution:**
Find the equation of the plane. Vectors: $\mathbf{a} = \langle -1, -2, 0 \rangle$, $\mathbf{b} = \langle -1, 0, 4 \rangle$.
$\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \langle -8, 4, -2 \rangle$. Simplify normal to $\langle 4, -2, 1 \rangle$.
Equation: $4x - 2y + z = 4(1) - 2(0) + 0 = 4$. So $z = 4 - 4x + 2y$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -4, \quad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = 2
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
dS = \sqrt{(-4)^2 + (2)^2 + 1} \, dA = \sqrt{16 + 4 + 1} \, dA = \sqrt{21} \, dA
$$
</MathWrapper>
Region $D$ in $xy$-plane: Connects $(1, 0)$ and $(0, -2)$. Line eq: $y - 0 = \frac{-2-0}{0-1}(x-1) \Rightarrow y = 2(x-1) = 2x - 2$.
Bounded by $x=0$, $y=0$, $y=2x-2$. Or $0 \le x \le 1$, $2x-2 \le y \le 0$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_S x \, dS &= \int_0^1 \int_{2x-2}^0 x \sqrt{21} \, dy \, dx \\
&= \sqrt{21} \int_0^1 x [y]_{2x-2}^0 \, dx \\
&= \sqrt{21} \int_0^1 x (0 - (2x - 2)) \, dx \\
&= \sqrt{21} \int_0^1 (2x - 2x^2) \, dx \\
&= \sqrt{21} \left[ x^2 - \frac{2}{3}x^3 \right]_0^1 \\
&= \sqrt{21} (1 - \frac{2}{3}) = \frac{\sqrt{21}}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
**Answer:** $\frac{\sqrt{21}}{3}$

**13.** Evaluate $\iint_S z^2 \, dS$, $S$ is the part of the paraboloid $x = y^2 + z^2$ given by $0 \le x \le 1$.

**Solution:**
This is a graph $x = g(y, z)$. We project onto the $yz$-plane.
$0 \le y^2 + z^2 \le 1$. $D$ is the unit disk in the $yz$-plane.
<MathWrapper>
$$
\frac{\partial x}{\partial y} = 2y, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial z} = 2z
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
dS = \sqrt{(2y)^2 + (2z)^2 + 1} \, dA = \sqrt{4(y^2+z^2) + 1} \, dA
$$
</MathWrapper>
Use polar coordinates for the $yz$-plane: $y = r \cos \theta, z = r \sin \theta$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
z^2 = r^2 \sin^2 \theta, \quad dS = \sqrt{4r^2 + 1} \, r \, dr \, d\theta
$$
</MathWrapper>
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_S z^2 \, dS &= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 (r^2 \sin^2 \theta) \sqrt{4r^2 + 1} \, r \, dr \, d\theta \\
&= \int_0^{2\pi} \sin^2 \theta \, d\theta \int_0^1 r^3 \sqrt{4r^2 + 1} \, dr
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Integral 1: $\int_0^{2\pi} \sin^2 \theta \, d\theta = \pi$.
Integral 2: Let $u = 4r^2 + 1$, then $du = 8r \, dr \Rightarrow r \, dr = \frac{1}{8} du$.
Since $r^2 = \frac{u-1}{4}$, $r^3 \, dr = r^2 \cdot r \, dr = \frac{u-1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{8} du = \frac{1}{32}(u-1) du$.
Limits: $r=0 \to u=1$, $r=1 \to u=5$.
<MathWrapper>
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_1^5 \frac{1}{32} (u-1) \sqrt{u} \, du &= \frac{1}{32} \int_1^5 (u^{3/2} - u^{1/2}) \, du \\
&= \frac{1}{32} \left[ \frac{2}{5} u^{5/2} - \frac{2}{3} u^{3/2} \right]_1^5 \\
&= \frac{1}{16} \left[ \left(\frac{1}{5} (5^{2}\sqrt{5}) - \frac{1}{3} (5\sqrt{5})\right) - \left(\frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{3}\right) \right] \\
&= \frac{1}{16} \left[ \left(5\sqrt{5} - \frac{5}{3}\sqrt{5}\right) - \left(\frac{3-5}{15}\right) \right] \\
&= \frac{1}{16} \left[ \frac{10\sqrt{5}}{3} + \frac{2}{15} \right]
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>
Total: $\pi \cdot \frac{1}{16} (\frac{50\sqrt{5} + 2}{15}) = \frac{\pi}{120} (25\sqrt{5} + 1)$.
(Re-checking arithmetic: $32 \times 15 = 480$. $2 \times 15 = 30$ in num? Wait.
$\frac{1}{16} [\frac{50\sqrt{5} + 2}{15}] = \frac{2(25\sqrt{5}+1)}{16 \cdot 15} = \frac{25\sqrt{5}+1}{8 \cdot 15} = \frac{25\sqrt{5}+1}{120}$. Correct.)
**Answer:** $\frac{\pi}{120}(1 + 25\sqrt{5})$
