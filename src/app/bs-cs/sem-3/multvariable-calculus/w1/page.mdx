# Functions of Several Variables: Limits and Continuity

**Book:** *Multivariable Calculus* by James Stewart, 9th Edition
**Chapter:** 14. Partial Derivatives
**Section:** 14.2 Limits and Continuity

---

## 1. Core Concepts

### Definition
Let $f$ be a function of two variables whose domain $D$ includes points arbitrarily close to $(a, b)$. Then we say that the **limit** of $f(x, y)$ as $(x, y)$ approaches $(a, b)$ is $L$ and we write:

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (a, b)} f(x, y) = L
$$

if for every number $\epsilon > 0$ there is a corresponding number $\delta > 0$ such that:

if $(x, y) \in D$ and $0 < \sqrt{(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2} < \delta$ then $|f(x, y) - L| < \epsilon$.

### Explanation of Approach
For functions of a single variable, when we let $x$ approach $a$, there are only two possible directions of approach, from the left or from the right. We recall that if $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \neq \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$, then $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ does not exist.

For functions of two variables, the situation is not as simple because we can let $(x, y)$ approach $(a, b)$ from an infinite number of directions in any manner whatsoever as long as $(x, y)$ stays within the domain of $f$.

Therefore, if the limit exists, then $f(x, y)$ must approach the same limit no matter how $(x, y)$ approaches $(a, b)$. Thus, if we find different paths of approach along which the function has different limits, then $\lim_{(x, y) \to (a, b)} f(x, y)$ does not exist.

---

## 2. Solved Example: Proving Non-Existence

**Example 1:** Show that $\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{x^2 - y^2}{x^2 + y^2}$ does not exist.

**Solution:**
Let $f(x, y) = \frac{x^2 - y^2}{x^2 + y^2}$.

**Path 1:** Approach $(0, 0)$ along the $x$-axis ($y = 0$).

$$
f(x, 0) = \frac{x^2 - 0}{x^2 + 0} = \frac{x^2}{x^2} = 1 \quad (\text{for all } x \neq 0)
$$

Thus, $f(x, y) \to 1$ as $(x, y) \to (0, 0)$ along the $x$-axis.

**Path 2:** Approach along the $y$-axis ($x = 0$).

$$
f(0, y) = \frac{0 - y^2}{0 + y^2} = \frac{-y^2}{y^2} = -1 \quad (\text{for all } y \neq 0)
$$

Thus, $f(x, y) \to -1$ as $(x, y) \to (0, 0)$ along the $y$-axis.

**Conclusion:** Since $f$ has two different limits as $(x, y)$ approaches $(0, 0)$ along two different lines, the given limit does not exist.

## 3. Exercises: Evaluating Limits
*The following questions involve finding the limit using continuity laws or simplification.*

**5.** Find the limit.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (3, 2)} (x^2y^3 - 4y^2)
$$

**Solution:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (3)^2(2)^3 - 4(2)^2 \\
& = (9)(8) - 4(4) \\
& = 72 - 16 \\
& = 56
\end{aligned}
$$

**Answer:** $56$

<br/>

**7.** Find the limit.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (-3, 1)} \frac{x^2y - xy^3}{x - y + 2}
$$

**Solution:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{(-3)^2(1) - (-3)(1)^3}{-3 - 1 + 2} \\
& = \frac{9(1) - (-3)}{-2} \\
& = \frac{9 + 3}{-2} \\
& = \frac{12}{-2} \\
& = -6
\end{aligned}
$$

**Answer:** $-6$

<br/>

**9.** Find the limit.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (\pi, \pi/2)} y \sin(x - y)
$$

**Solution:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{\pi}{2} \sin\left(\pi - \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \\
& = \frac{\pi}{2} \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \\
& = \frac{\pi}{2} (1) \\
& = \frac{\pi}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Answer:** $\frac{\pi}{2}$

<br/>

**10.** Find the limit.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (3, 2)} e^{2x - y}
$$

**Solution:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = e^{2(3) - 2} \\
& = e^{6 - 2} \\
& = e^{4}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Answer:** $e^4$

<br/>

**11.** Find the limit.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (1, 1)} \frac{x^2y^3 - x^3y^2}{x - y}
$$

**Solution:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{(x, y) \to (1, 1)} \frac{x^2y^3 - x^3y^2}{x - y} 
& = \lim_{(x, y) \to (1, 1)} \frac{x^2y^2(y - x)}{x - y} \\
& = \lim_{(x, y) \to (1, 1)} \frac{-x^2y^2(x - y)}{x - y} \\
& = \lim_{(x, y) \to (1, 1)} -x^2y^2 \\
& = -(1)^2(1)^2 \\
& = -1
\end{aligned}
$$

**Answer:** $-1$

## 4. Exercises: Proving Limits Do Not Exist
*The following questions require testing different paths (usually axes or the line $y=x$) to show the results differ.*

**13.** Show that the limit does not exist.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{y^2}{x^2 + y^2}
$$

**Solution:**
*Path 1:* Along the $x$-axis ($y=0$).

$$
\lim_{(x, 0) \to (0, 0)} \frac{0^2}{x^2 + 0} = \lim_{x \to 0} 0 = 0
$$

*Path 2:* Along the $y$-axis ($x=0$).

$$
\lim_{(0, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{y^2}{0 + y^2} = \lim_{y \to 0} 1 = 1
$$

Since $0 \neq 1$, the limit does not exist.

<br/>

**14.** Show that the limit does not exist.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{2xy}{x^2 + 3y^2}
$$

**Solution:**
*Path 1:* Along the $x$-axis ($y=0$).

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x(0)}{x^2 + 0} = 0
$$

*Path 2:* Along the line $y=x$.

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x(x)}{x^2 + 3(x)^2} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x^2}{4x^2} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

Since $0 \neq \frac{1}{2}$, the limit does not exist.

<br/>

**15.** Show that the limit does not exist.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{(x + y)^2}{x^2 + y^2}
$$

**Solution:**
*Path 1:* Along the $x$-axis ($y=0$).

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{(x + 0)^2}{x^2 + 0} = 1
$$

*Path 2:* Along the line $y=x$.

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{(x + x)^2}{x^2 + x^2} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{4x^2}{2x^2} = 2
$$

Since $1 \neq 2$, the limit does not exist.

<br/>

**17.** Show that the limit does not exist.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{y^2 \sin^2 x}{x^4 + y^4}
$$

**Solution:**
*Path 1:* Along the $x$-axis ($y=0$).

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{0}{x^4} = 0
$$

*Path 2:* Along the line $y=x$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x^2 \sin^2 x}{x^4 + x^4} & = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x^2 \sin^2 x}{2x^4} \\
& = \frac{1}{2} \lim_{x \to 0} \left(\frac{\sin x}{x}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1)^2 = \frac{1}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $0 \neq \frac{1}{2}$, the limit does not exist.

## 5. Exercises: Advanced Graphs and Curves
*These questions involve specific curves to prove non-existence.*

**35.** Use a graph or level curves to explain why the limit does not exist.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{2x^2 + 3xy + 4y^2}{3x^2 + 5y^2}
$$

**Solution:**
*Path 1:* Along the $x$-axis ($y=0$).

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x^2}{3x^2} = \frac{2}{3}
$$

*Path 2:* Along the $y$-axis ($x=0$).

$$
\lim_{y \to 0} \frac{4y^2}{5y^2} = \frac{4}{5}
$$

Since $\frac{2}{3} \neq \frac{4}{5}$, the limit does not exist.

<br/>

**36.** Use a graph or level curves to explain why the limit does not exist.

$$
\lim_{(x, y) \to (0, 0)} \frac{xy^3}{x^2 + y^6}
$$

**Solution:**
*Path 1:* Along the $y$-axis ($x=0$).

$$
\lim_{y \to 0} \frac{0}{y^6} = 0
$$

*Path 2:* Along the curve $x = y^3$.

$$
\lim_{y \to 0} \frac{(y^3)(y^3)}{(y^3)^2 + y^6} = \lim_{y \to 0} \frac{y^6}{y^6 + y^6} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

Since $0 \neq \frac{1}{2}$, the limit does not exist.
